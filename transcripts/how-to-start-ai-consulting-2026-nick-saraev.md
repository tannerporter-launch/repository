## Marketing [00:51]

At the front end of any business — whether an AI consulting agency or something else — this is where you want to focus all of your energy and attention, because if you don't have leads, you don't have a business; you barely even have a hobby. If you're at $0 in revenue and spending any of your time on anything that is not directly generating leads, you are, as a rule, wasting time. My recommendation: quit procrastinating. Let's break down the four major marketing mechanisms that actually work for beginners today: **platform sales, cold email, communities, and cold DMs.** I'll give you assets for every one. Each method comes with trade-offs, so I'll rank them across three axes: the **time** investment required, the **capital** investment required, and the **technical barrier to entry.**

## Sales [08:17]

Say your lead generation is running well and you're booking meetings consistently. Now it's time to sell. Here's the frame to remember: **sales today is not about tricking people.** It's about uncovering their pain, showing how your product solves that pain, and positioning yourself as the obvious investment. That's it.

I use a very simple sales structure that I've taught to over 6,000 people, many of whom have used it to close deals worth as much as **$60,000 upfront**. It runs about 30–45 minutes. The structure: **build rapport → set the schedule → discovery → solutions → investment → send a proposal.**

### 1. Rapport
At all my companies I've adhered to the idea that rapport matters — people tend to buy from people they like. Some disagree; that's fine. Our first job is simple: **get them to smile.** I usually do this with a light joke or compliment. Most of the time I just ask, "Hey, where are you based?" and if it's somewhere I know — "No way, I love that city. How long have you been living there?" It's not rocket science; it's social lubricant, and it can take as little as 10 seconds. Then transition: "All right, you ready to jump in?"

### 2. Set the schedule
This is where it starts getting important. Lay out a road map and frame the call — take control and show them you have a defined structure. Literally say: *"Here's how these calls usually go. First, I want to understand why you booked. Then I want to dig deeper into your business, maybe chat some numbers. Then, if I think we can help, I'll show you what that looks like and we'll talk about next steps. Sound good?"* You're just setting the frame — telling them what you're going to do, and then doing it.

### 3. Discovery
This is probably the most important part of the call. If you simply ask the questions you want answered, the client will hand you all the ammunition you need to close. Good examples:
- **"Why did you decide to book the call?"** — Probably the highest-ROI question ever, and almost nobody asks it: "Peter, you get a lot of cold emails every day; why did you book with me and not the other 20 people? What about my offer made you say yes?" Ask every person this and you're 70–80% of the way there.
- **"What is the goal right now?"** — If there's no north star, you're often wasting your time. Once they tell you their goals, you get to frame everything you do in pursuit of that goal.
- **"Assuming we fix this for you, what do you think it'll do for the company?"** — They'll tell you the impact: "We'll make tons more money," or "This is costing us 30 grand a month; fix it and you solve that leak," or "We're spending $3,000/month per person on three staff; build this and we could let them all go." Now you know, at a bird's-eye view, the outcome of the system you're proposing — and as long as you can frame it as a positive ROI, you're golden.
- **"Why haven't you done this yet?"** — "What's stopped you until now? You've told me this has been a problem for six months — why are we only acting on it now?"

You just ask questions and gather the data you need to close them later. Enterprise consultants dress discovery up to sound fancy, but it's basically: "Tell me where to shoot, and I'll shoot." Also ask **follow-ups** relevant to their situation — if their goal is to close more clients, ask, "How much is a client paying when they come in the door?" You're building a wider perspective on their pain points, and it shows you're an active listener. This is the heart of sales.

### 4. Propose the solution
Once you have the full picture, position your system or product as the logical solution to their pain: *"We've solved many similar problems before, and this is a great fit. To make a long story short, we'll solve X by doing Y, and my hope is it saves you Z dollars a month. Would you like to see an example of how this works?"* Because systems are visual, give them a demonstration. Keep a **system library** of five or ten systems you routinely sell, ready to demo on demand. The benefit: it simplifies delivery later, because you're showing systems you already have templates for. If they say, "I really like that," you just duplicate the template and spend 15 minutes touching it up. This is **templated, productized sales** — starting at the end and working backwards.

### 5. Frame the investment
Finally, after building all that value, you drop the price. Always do this **at the end**: by then you've built tremendous value in the customer's eyes. Drop it at the beginning and they fixate on the number while you spend the whole call justifying it. (If someone asks point-blank and seems ready to buy, I'll usually answer — but otherwise I keep building value until they're almost asking me: "Dude, just tell me how much this costs already, it sounds perfect.")

A good framing: *"Hey, based on what you told me — to save you that $50,000 price tag — our project would cost $19,000. Assuming there's nothing else to add on, your investment would pay for itself in literally the first month."* That's powerful because they already told you the size of their pain; you just put two and two together. Two important points for beginners: **(1)** always frame it as an **investment**; **(2)** when you drop the price, **shut up** — it's the only tense, high-risk part of the call, and you navigate it by saying nothing. First person to speak loses. Say "$19,000," then wait.

**Objection handling.** You'll get objections; handling them is a learned skill I can't fully teach in a few minutes. High-level tips:
- If someone wants it cheaper, **don't just discount** — remove line items or functionality, so you're not reducing your price or value, and the client has to decide which parts they value most (which also gives you more info).
- A vague **"I need to think about it"** is almost never the real reason. They have an objection; you probably didn't build enough value. Go one level deeper: "That makes sense — would this system solve the needs you put in front of me?" If **no**, you've surfaced the real objection and can handle it on the spot. If **yes**, there's little more you can do; send the proposal and move on.
- If someone won't decide right then, **that's okay.** A lot of high-ticket salespeople try to force a decision on the call; don't. We're consultants, not car salespeople — our margins aren't 3%. If they need to talk to their spouse, fine: tell them you'll send a detailed proposal that writes everything out verbatim, give them the time they need, and set your proposal to follow up automatically.

### 6. Send the proposal
Because the systems we sell are technical, you need something **written down**. Any expectation mismatch can lead to bloated scopes and wasted time. Always have a proposal with a scope (I have a previous video all about proposals). Most proposal platforms — **PandaDoc**, Better Proposals, etc. — now have automatic follow-up: fire the proposal off and have it follow up over the next week or two, optionally with a time-limited offer. With this "layered" approach (not closing directly on the call), you'll miss closing *some* clients you could have closed — but the people you end up working with tend to prefer it, because they made both a logical *and* an emotional decision, not just an emotional one on the spot. For technical work, that matters. Script's in the description.

## Onboarding [18:21]

After you close the deal and collect payment, you need a process to onboard the client into your ecosystem. **Onboarding sets the tone for the entire relationship.** Get it wrong and you create endless headaches and tank your odds of upselling or retaining them. Nail it and you look professional and organized, and your odds of making more money go way up. That's why you **front-load everything into a kickoff call** (my SOP is in the description). A good kickoff goes: **introduction → goals & timelines → communication → platform access & 2FA.**

### 1. Introduction & framing
One minute, tops — similar to "setting the schedule" on the sales call. Thank them for joining, cover who's who, and set the frame: *"My goal by the end of this call is for our company to have everything we need, so we don't have to bother you for permissions every day for the next week. As someone who's done many of these, the biggest issue in this space is logistics — 2FA codes, passwords, access — and these become a nightmare if they drag out. So instead of chasing them for weeks, I want to handle it live, right now. I'll also cover timelines, how we'll communicate, and walk through any platforms I need to sign up for. Sound good?"* They'll say yes, and you've built instant trust and social proof, and shown you have a dedicated process for the exact problems that plague other freelancers.

### 2. Goals & timelines
Lay out exactly what will happen over the engagement — how long things take, what they can expect at each step: *"Our goal is to start today and wrap up within three weeks; if we can deliver earlier, we will. The project splits into three clear milestones so you always know where things stand."* Clients love this — odds are they've been burned by consultants who start and then vanish into a black hole. You're showing them they won't have to chase you.

### 3. Communication expectations
*"You'll get one daily update, Monday to Friday, over Slack / email / WhatsApp. It'll include where the project's at, what's next, and any blockers I need help with. If you have one-off questions, I'll batch them and cover them in that daily check-in. Does that work?"* This one move saves ~90% of the back-and-forth chaos in any engagement. If you don't set firm boundaries (which beginners usually don't know how to do), clients swallow a disproportionate amount of your time with small questions, random calls, and "I had an idea." Build your own structure: anything that comes up between updates gets covered at the next update. Now the relationship is on **your** terms.

### 4. Platform access
A lot of people screw this up — they send a list of logins and wait weeks. Instead (and what 6,000+ people in Maker School now do), handle it **live on the call**: *"Today we need admin access to your Slack and admin access to your Asana — let's set both up right now. If a 2FA code pops up, just read it to me. We can get this done in the next three minutes. Sound cool?"* What would've taken 20 emails or a weird 3-minute window between 9:00 and 9:03 p.m., you front-load here. And because the client signed up to the platforms, there's **no handoff** later — no jumping into their workspace to re-set-up and duplicate everything; it's already there. Saves enormous time.

### 5. Wrap-up
A quick check-in: ask if they have questions, then confirm: "Great — let's kick this off. I'll send your first update tomorrow." That closes the loop and reassures them you're off to the races. By the end of that 30-minute call, the client knows exactly how you work, you have everything you need, and there's zero silly back-and-forth.

## Fulfillment [23:12]

Fulfillment is the fun part — actually doing the work. But know this: **fulfillment is not about delivering the system; it's about managing the perception of the engagement.** If your client doesn't *feel* like progress is happening, they'll assume it isn't. If they can't see what you've built (or you're not telling them), they'll value it far less. So break fulfillment into two parts: **communication** and **delivery.**

### Communication
Keep it simple — clients don't want long novels; they want clear, concise actions. Use simple templates (in the description), like a daily check-in, Monday–Friday, over Slack: *"Hey [client], just checking in — no action needed from your end. We just finished X, just started Y, and I'll update you tomorrow. Thanks!"* Each takes ~60 seconds per client and builds massive trust, substantially improving your ability to upsell later, because you've established professionalism and shown you know how to interact with busy, high-net-worth people: give them bullet-point overviews, and if they need to take an action, spell it out.

At a certain point in services, the important thing is **not** actually delivering the service — anyone can deliver the service. Your ability to deliver isn't what differentiates you; **your ability to manage the perception of that service better than anyone else** is. With zero change to the actual service — just communicating every day, mindfully and professionally — you can basically double your prices. Your lifetime values will roughly double.

### Delivery
Delivery is as much about presenting the thing the right way as doing it. Whether it's an automated invoicing system, a LinkedIn scraper, or a Slack workflow, the highest-performing agencies **package systems like products.** They don't just hand over a Make.com scenario and call it a day — they treat it like a **diamond**: you don't just get a diamond, you get it in a box, wrapped in satin, with a bow and a card. Those things manage the perception of the project's value.

It takes a few minutes after the project, serves as a record of the outcome, and — most importantly — lets you manage the perception of the delivery. ## Retention [26:46]

Retention is the process of turning a one-off project into a long-term relationship and significantly increasing the lifetime value of an engagement. Very few consultants do it — unfortunate for them, fortunate for us. If you did great work and your client is really happy — happy enough to thank you for it — the last thing you should do is disappear and wait for them to call you back. That's leaving so much money on the table; it's like hitting a home run and forgetting to run the bases.

The best time to sell a client on a follow-up project is **right after you've wowed them** with the current one — that's when they're most excited and trusting, and when you can leverage **reciprocity.** The key: **over-deliver just a little.** Don't only deliver what you promised — go one step further and deliver an extra 5%: *"Here's your Slack workflow — and while I was in there I noticed X, so I fixed that too. Plus, here's some documentation."* That triggers reciprocity, which you then leverage by pitching a follow-up project — significantly improving the odds they say yes.

It sounds like: *"While I was setting up your Slack system, I noticed your funnel wasn't sending SMS messages — interesting, because I've worked with a bunch of people doing seven and eight figures and they all send SMS. I'm thinking if you fixed that, you might add another $10–15K a month to your bottom line. I know it's a bold ask, but would you be open to me just showing you how to do this, whether or not you work with me?"* Trigger that reciprocity and clients tend to love it, so your odds of getting them onto a closing call — for a one-off project or a managed service — go way up, and you can make a ton more.
